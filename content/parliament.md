🏛️ The Parliament of India (Articles 79–122)
The Parliament is the supreme legislative body of the Republic of India. According to Article 79, the Parliament consists of:

The President (Though not a member of either House, he is an integral part).

Council of States (Rajya Sabha).

House of the People (Lok Sabha).

🏗️ 1. Composition of the Two Houses
A. Rajya Sabha (Upper House / Council of States)
Max Strength: 250 (Currently 245).

Membership: 238 are representatives of States/UTs + 12 Nominated by the President (Arts, Science, Literature, Social Service).

Nature: A Permanent Body. It cannot be dissolved.

Tenure: Members are elected for 6 years, with 1/3rd retiring every second year.

Election: Indirect election by elected members of State Legislative Assemblies (PRSTV system).

B. Lok Sabha (Lower House / House of the People)
Max Strength: 550 (After the removal of Anglo-Indian nomination by the 104th Amendment).

Membership: Directly elected by the people on the basis of Universal Adult Franchise.

Nature: Not a permanent body. Normal term is 5 years.

Tenure: Can be dissolved by the President or extended during a National Emergency (1 year at a time).

⚖️ 2. Qualifications & Disqualifications
Qualifications (Article 84):
Citizen of India.

Age: 25 years for Lok Sabha | 30 years for Rajya Sabha.

Must be registered as an elector.

Disqualifications (Article 102 & 10th Schedule):
Holding an Office of Profit.

Unsound mind or undischarged insolvent.

Anti-Defection Law (10th Schedule): If a member voluntarily gives up party membership or votes against party whip.

🛠️ 3. Officers of Parliament
The Speaker (Lok Sabha):
Elected by the Lok Sabha from amongst its members.

Does not vote in the first instance but has a Casting Vote in case of a tie.

Decides whether a bill is a Money Bill (His decision is final).

Presides over a Joint Sitting of both houses.

The Chairman (Rajya Sabha):
The Vice-President of India is the Ex-officio Chairman.

Unlike the Speaker, he is not a member of the house he presides over.

📜 4. Legislative Procedures (Types of Bills)
1. Ordinary Bill (Article 107 & 108)
Can be introduced in either house.

In case of a deadlock, the President can call a Joint Sitting.

2. Money Bill (Article 110)
Can only be introduced in Lok Sabha with the President's recommendation.

Rajya Sabha has restricted powers; it must return the bill within 14 days. It cannot reject or amend it.

3. Financial Bill (Article 117)
Deals with fiscal matters but is different from a Money Bill.

4. Constitutional Amendment Bill (Article 368)
Must be passed by both houses separately with a special majority. No provision for Joint Sitting.

⏳ 5. Parliamentary Sessions & Devices
Summoning: The President calls the houses to meet. The gap between two sessions cannot exceed 6 months.

Quorum: Minimum 1/10th of the total members must be present to conduct business.

Question Hour: The first hour of every parliamentary sitting (Starred, Unstarred, and Short Notice questions).

Zero Hour: Starts immediately after Question Hour (Indian innovation, not in the rules).

Adjournment vs Prorogation:

Adjournment: Suspends work for a few hours/days (Done by Presiding Officer).

Prorogation: Ends the entire session (Done by President).

🔍 6. Parliamentary Committees
Since Parliament is too big to handle all details, it works through committees:

Public Accounts Committee (PAC): Examines the report of the CAG. 22 members (15 LS, 7 RS).

Estimates Committee: Largest committee (30 members, all from Lok Sabha).

Committee on Public Undertakings: Examines reports of PSUs.

💡 7. Exclusive Powers of Rajya Sabha (Special Powers)
Article 249: Can authorize Parliament to make laws on a subject in the State List.

Article 312: Can authorize the creation of new All-India Services
